A 2d20 game needs very little hardware. Each player has a character sheet, a pencil, and access to a small pool of dice. The gamemaster needs the rulebook, some scratch paper, and a couple of tokens to mark Momentum and Doom as they rise and fall.

## What you need

**Dice.** Each player should have two twenty-sided dice (d20) within easy reach, and the table should share a pool of six-sided dice for damage and Combat Dice. Five or six d6 between everyone usually suffices; in busy fights you may want more. The d6 used for damage is treated as a special die — see [The Core Mechanic]({{ '/core-mechanic/' | relative_url }}) for how its faces are read.

**Tokens.** Two distinct kinds of physical markers, one color for Momentum and another for Doom. Glass beads, poker chips, or coins all work. A handful of twenty of each is plenty for a session.

**Sheets.** A printed character sheet per player. The reverse side is useful for noting equipment, contacts, and notes between sessions.

## The shape of a session

A typical session moves through three modes, and the rules behave a little differently in each:

- **Narrative time.** Free conversation, exploration, social scenes. The gamemaster calls for a skill test when there is meaningful risk, and otherwise the fiction proceeds. Most of the session usually happens here.
- **Action scenes.** When initiative matters — a fight, a chase, an escape from a collapsing tomb — the game switches to a structured turn order with explicit zones and reactions. See [Action Scenes]({{ '/action-scenes/' | relative_url }}).
- **Downtime.** Between adventures. Characters spend Gold, heal wounds, mend relationships, and undertake long-term projects. The rules are lighter here and largely the gamemaster's call.

## Roles at the table

The **players** each run a single character — their hero in the world. They describe what their character intends to do, roll dice when called upon, and spend Momentum to push their successes further.

The **gamemaster** runs everything else: the supporting cast, the monsters, the weather, the gods. They set difficulties, narrate consequences, and manage the Doom pool that the world spends to push back against the heroes.

## A first session in one paragraph

Hand out pre-generated characters or walk the table through [character creation]({{ '/character-creation/' | relative_url }}). Open with a brief scene that gives each character something to react to. When a player describes an action whose outcome is genuinely uncertain, call for a skill test (see [The Core Mechanic]({{ '/core-mechanic/' | relative_url }})). Track Momentum on a shared pile in the middle of the table; track Doom on your side of the screen. When swords come out, run an action scene. End the session on a hook, not a resolution — give the players something to talk about until next time.

  <p class="callout-title">A note on tone</p>
  <p>The Hyborian Age rewards a particular register at the table: terse, physical, decadent. Players tend to enjoy the game more when the gamemaster narrates concrete sensory detail (the smell of the temple, the weight of the axe) rather than abstract stakes ("you feel uneasy"). The system supports this — it rewards risky, declarative action — but the table sets the tone.</p>
