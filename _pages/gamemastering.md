This page is the one to read end-to-end before your first session. The rest of the document is for the players' eyes as much as yours; this one is the gamemaster's craft notes.

## Your job at the table

The 2d20 system pushes the gamemaster toward a particular role: not the impartial arbiter of every die roll, but the active opponent of the heroes in a structured economy of risk. You set difficulties; you spend Doom to push back; you decide what counts as a scene and when it ends. You are also, simultaneously, the supportive narrator who is rooting for the heroes to do something memorable.

The two halves of the role coexist. Spend Doom hard when the heroes have earned a complication; spend it gently when the table needs a win. The system gives you both levers — your judgment is the one piece of the game it cannot supply.

## Setting difficulties

The instinct of a new gamemaster is to set every test at D2 or D3, "to make it interesting." The rules push back on this: **the default is D1.** A capable hero, competent at the thing they're attempting, should succeed at D1 most of the time. D2 means *the situation is making this hard*; D3 means *the situation is genuinely against you.*

A useful internal checklist when picking difficulty:

1. **What is this character good at?** A character with Expertise 3 and Focus 2 is supposed to make this look easy. Respect that.
2. **What is the fictional friction?** Concrete: bad light, slippery footing, hostile observers, a closing window. Each piece of friction is +1 to difficulty.
3. **What's at stake?** A scene-ending test (the door that has to open right now, the leap across the chasm) is the wrong time to be punitive. Let the rolls land; let the Momentum drive the next moment.

If you ever catch yourself setting a test at D3+ "because it should be hard," ask the players for the inputs and let the dice be the only randomness. A D1 test with three named complications layered on the fail is more dramatic than a D4 with no narrative texture.

## When to call for a test

Not every action needs a die. Call for a test when:

- The outcome is *uncertain*.
- The outcome *matters*.
- Failure is *interesting* — not just a wall.

If the answer to any of those is no, narrate the outcome. If a test fails and you don't immediately know what happens, your difficulty was wrong, not the dice.

## Failing forward

When a test fails, the scene should always *go somewhere*. The wrong way to handle a failed *Stealth* test is "the guard sees you, nothing happens." The right ways:

- The guard sees you and now you fight; or
- You make it past, but you've made noise and the *next* guard is alerted; or
- You make it past, but a piece of your gear is caught on the bars and you must leave it; or
- The guard sees you but assumes you're someone else — for now.

Doom is your tool here. Spend 1 Doom to put a stress on a successful action; spend 2 to escalate a failed one.

## Spending Doom

Doom is what the world spends to be the world. Spend it when:

- An enemy genuinely needs an extra die to land a meaningful blow.
- A complication is about to escalate from inconvenient to consequential.
- A scene is dragging and needs a *thing to happen* (reinforcements, the floor giving way, a new face at the door).
- The pool is large and the climax is here.

Do not spend Doom when:

- The scene's outcome is already settled.
- The heroes are losing and you're piling on for sport.
- The spend doesn't change the player's decisions for the next round or two.

The rule of thumb: **a Doom spend should make at least one player at the table say "okay, now what."**

## Scenes, sessions, adventures

The game's rhythm operates at three scales.

- A **scene** is a unit of attention — one location, one purpose, a few minutes to an hour of play. Momentum drains at scene's end; banked Momentum belongs to the scene that earned it.
- A **session** is a unit of play. Doom typically resets at the start, though a campaign with continuity may carry some Doom over. XP rewards happen at session's end (see below).
- An **adventure** is a unit of story — a few sessions resolving a single arc. Fortune resets, characters heal completely, downtime happens here.

When framing a scene: pick a *clock* (what runs out?), a *threat* (what wants the heroes to fail?), and an *opportunity* (what can they gain?). A scene with all three runs itself.

## Experience and rewards

Default XP per session is in the range of **400–600**, with adjustments for length and intensity. A useful split:

- **300** — base attendance.
- **+100** — for completing a major scene or beat.
- **+100** — for a moment of distinctive roleplay (the player leaned into a flaw, made a hard choice, did something memorable).
- **+50–100** — for *learning something* about the world (new contact, new lore, a clue uncovered).

Spend ladder, repeated from [Character Creation]({{ '/character-creation/' | relative_url }}):

| Improvement                | XP   |
| -------------------------- | ---- |
| New skill talent (no prereq) | 200 |
| New skill talent with prereq | 400 |
| +1 Expertise in a skill    | 200 (max 5) |
| +1 Focus in a skill        | 300 (max 3, ≤ Expertise) |
| New spell                  | 200 |
| New language               | 100 |
| +1 to an attribute         | 800 (rare; story-justified) |

## The Doom budget

A useful internal model: at the start of a session, decide roughly **how much Doom you intend to spend** by session's end — typically 8–15 for a full session of dangerous play. Spend less and the heroes will feel unchallenged; spend more and you risk a TPK.

Track your spending over the night. If you're at session's halfway point with no Doom spent, the pacing is probably off — the heroes haven't faced a real threat yet. If you're already empty and there's an hour left, you've spent everything early and the climax will lack teeth.

## Encouraging the right play

The system is most fun when players spend Momentum aggressively and the gamemaster spends Doom decisively. A few table cues:

- Read the Momentum pool aloud at the end of each round. "You have 4 banked, dropping to 3 next round."
- When a player asks "what does this do?", offer concrete spends from the menu rather than open-ended invitation.
- When you spend Doom, *show the cost*. "I'm spending 2 Doom to bring in another two Asshuri from the side hall." Make the economy visible.

## A note on the source material

The Hyborian Age, at its best, has a particular tonal range: physicality, decadence, terse menace, mythic stakes. The mechanics support that — the system rewards bold action, punishes hesitation, and is willing to kill characters who push their luck once too often. As a gamemaster, lean into that texture. Describe the sweat, the candle smoke, the weight of the gold. The dice will do the rest.

## Quick reference

- **Default difficulty is D1.** Modify up only for explicit fictional friction.
- **Call for a roll when failure is interesting**; otherwise, narrate.
- **Doom budget per session:** ~8–15. Spend with intent.
- **Each scene needs a clock, a threat, and an opportunity.**
- **Make the economy visible** to the players.
