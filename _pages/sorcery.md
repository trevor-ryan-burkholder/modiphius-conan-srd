Sorcery in the Hyborian Age is not the gentle craft of a fantasy world. It is slow, costly, contagious, and morally suspect. A sorcerer's power grows by binding themselves to forces that do not return their care, and the price of casting is paid in mind, soul, and the future of the people around them. Mechanically, the rules reflect this: spells are not the system's default way to solve a problem, and a sorcerer who casts often will burn out fast.

## Becoming a sorcerer

A character is a sorcerer if they have **Expertise 1 or more in *Sorcery*** and at least one **Sorcery spell** known. Most spells require a *patron* — a name, a tradition, a teacher, or a forbidden text — that the character is bound to. Patrons grant access to specific spell lists and impose specific costs.

The four broad traditions:

- **The Esoteric** — academic sorcery from old libraries; least costly in price, slowest in power. Cosmopolitan sorcerers (Khitai, Stygia's tower-mages).
- **The Bargained** — spells acquired by pact with a named outsider. Big effects, terrible long-term consequences.
- **The Inherited** — bloodline magic. Always-on, hard to refuse. The bearers rarely live well.
- **The Hungry** — spells that draw on a fixed external source (a relic, a place, a leyline). Powerful at home, gutted away from it.

A character may belong to more than one tradition, but most spells are tied to one of them and require that the caster have the relevant standing.

## Learning a spell

A new spell is purchased with experience at the standard rate (typically **200 XP** per spell, more for advanced spells with prerequisites). Spells often require:

- The relevant patron / tradition.
- A minimum *Sorcery* Expertise.
- A teacher, a text, or an experience that justifies the new knowledge.

The gamemaster has wide latitude to make the acquisition itself an adventure. A spell *should* feel like a story-worthy thing to have learned.

## Casting a spell

Casting is a skill test: **Sorcery (Willpower)** against a difficulty set by the spell. The procedure:

1. **Declare the spell** and its target.
2. **Pay the casting cost** — typically a number of Resolve points, plus optional Doom to amplify or accelerate the spell.
3. **Roll the test.** Difficulty is set by the spell, modified by circumstance (interrupted casting, foreign tradition, unwilling target, missing components).
4. **On success**, the spell takes effect. Spend Momentum to extend duration, area, intensity, or to suppress the gamemaster's complications.
5. **On failure**, the cost is still paid. The gamemaster may spend Doom to inflict a *backlash* — an effect comparable to the spell's intended one, but turned against the caster or their allies.
6. **On a natural 20 (complication)**, the spell may go off but with a sorcerous consequence — a watcher takes notice, the caster gains a *Trauma*, a piece of the spell sticks and persists where it should not.

## Casting costs

Most spells cost some combination of:

| Cost type            | Description                                                                          |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Resolve**          | Direct cost paid from the caster's mental track. Heavy spells drain Resolve fast.    |
| **Doom**             | Adds to the gamemaster's pool. Some spells *require* Doom; others let you trade.     |
| **Time**             | Many real spells take a Standard Action; ritual spells take minutes, hours, or days. |
| **Material**         | Components (incense, blood, a sacrificed animal, a specific relic). Spell-dependent. |
| **Reputation / Soul** | Long-term cost: a sorcerer's standing in society sours; the world watches them differently. |

A typical "petty" spell might cost 1 Resolve and a Standard Action. A major ritual might cost an hour, three named components, 4 Resolve, and 2 Doom from the gamemaster's pool — and that's just to cast it once.

## Petty enchantments

Beyond the main spell list, sorcerers may perform **petty enchantments** — minor magical effects that any sorcerer can attempt: a small light, a brief charm on a coin, a moment of dread cast on a hireling. These cost little (often 1 Resolve, sometimes nothing) and are difficulty **D0–D1**. They are the parlor tricks of a sorcerer, and they are what makes the character feel magical between major castings.

The gamemaster has wide latitude on what counts as a petty enchantment. A useful test: if the effect is *narratively interesting* but *mechanically modest*, allow it.

## Common spell shapes

A non-exhaustive sketch of the kinds of effect you'll see, so the table can read a new spell on sight:

- **Curse** — single target; impose a difficulty step, a permanent fear, or a temporary debility.
- **Charm / Influence** — bend a target's will toward a single instruction; lasts moments to hours.
- **Summoning** — call something from elsewhere; usually requires a bound circle, time, and components.
- **Divination** — ask a question of the unseen; the answer is rarely complete and never free.
- **Necromancy** — raise, command, or speak with the dead. Reliably draws unwelcome attention.
- **Warding** — protect a place, a person, or a passage from a defined intruder for a duration.
- **Transformation** — change a target's shape or aspect, often partially and rarely cleanly.
- **Direct harm** — strike with cold, fire, blood, or pure malign attention. Costliest in Resolve.

## Sorcerous danger

Three things make sorcery dangerous beyond the dice:

1. **It is contagious.** Spells leave traces; powerful magic draws other practitioners. A serious working is rarely a secret for long.
2. **It marks the caster.** A sorcerer accumulates *signs* — a creeping pallor, eyes that pick up firelight wrong, dreams that other sleepers can sometimes overhear. These are roleplay first, mechanical second.
3. **The dead never quite stay buried.** A killed sorcerer is not safe to be near, in this setting. Their patron may resent the interruption.

## Quick reference

- **Sorcery (Willpower)** test, difficulty set by spell.
- **Cost** = Resolve ± Doom, possibly time and components.
- **Failure costs you the price anyway**, and may incur backlash.
- **Petty enchantments** are cheap, fast, low-difficulty; the parlor magic of a real sorcerer.
- **A complication on a sorcery roll is always bad** — never just narrative texture.
